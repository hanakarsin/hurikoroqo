University of Nairobi loses 3 staff members to COVID-19

- The institution's vice-chancellor said the three staff members succumbed to COVID-19 within a period of three weeks

- Further reports showed two other staff members had tested positive for the virus

- According to the VC, the three had been working from home at the time they contracted the virus and died

- Kiama urged the institution's staff to consider working from home while asking those who were 58 years and above to take leave

The University of Nairobi's Vice-Chancellor Stephen Kiama announced on Friday, July 10, the institution had lost three of its staff to COVID-19 within three weeks

Kiama said the affected staff had been working from home and that two others had tested positive for the virus as cases of the disease increase in the country.

The vice-chancellor announced the staff's deaths when he addressed staff and students in a briefing that was also streamed live on the institution’s website.

“These are not mere statistics. These are our brothers, sisters, parents and friends. The University of Nairobi has also not been spared by this enemy, the COVID-19,” he said.

Further reports by The Standard indicated Kiama had insisted that staff had to work from home and those who were 58 years and above to take leave.

“We continue to insist that staff must work from home. Those who are 58 years and above can take leave and that each one must keep safe wherever they are,” Kiama was quoted.

The development further complicated the planned reopening of higher learning institutions and colleges as earlier indicated by the Ministry of Education.

“We have issued our advisory that September 2020, opening will be impossible because we expect cases to peak on November 2020. We shall not take risks as yet,” said Kiama.

Education Cabinet Secretary George Magoha had said the reopening of universities for face-to-face sessions would be based on approved compliance with the Ministry of Health Covid-19 protocols.

The CS noted that teacher training colleges and TVET institutions would be allowed to reopen from September 2020, subject to strict adherence to the ministry’s Covid-19 protocols.

However, he urged universities and colleges to continue holding virtual learning.

“Universities should consider phased reopening to achieve physical and social distancing, especially in halls of residence, lecture rooms and dining halls,” said Magoha.

Vice-Chancellors Committee chairperson Geoffrey Muluvi said each institution will make their own opening arrangements and put in place health measures to be approved by the ministry.

Prior reports showed the university's staff had been very actively involved in the fight against the COVID-19 pandemic as researchers were working towards finding a solution to the pandemic.

Kiama had said several university lecturers are working with the government, giving advice and educating Kenyans on various media platforms about the pandemic.

He said faculty and postgraduate students at the College of Health Sciences continued to offer services at Kenyatta National Hospital (KNH) and other hospitals in the country.

Kiama said the institution’s senate in a meeting underscored the university was being guided by the philosophy of the need to connect and inspire Kenyans by providing leadership.
