Gov't will Compensate Kenyans for Permanent Damage or Death from Vaccine, Patrick Amoth

- Patrick Amoth confirmed that the Russian-developed vaccine Sputnik V was in the country

- He said the government was trying to put in place indemnification measures for private companies importing other vaccines

- According to Amoth, COVID-19 Vaccines Global Access has set aside money for severe damages and deaths caused by vaccines distributed through its platform

The Ministry of Health has said money has been set aside to compensate Kenyans who will suffer permanent damage or lose their lives due to the COVID-19 vaccine.

Patrick Amoth, the acting director-general for the Health of Health, said COVID-19 Vaccines Global Access, (COVAX) had set aside money for such unfortunate incidents caused by vaccines distributed through its platform.

Amoth, who was appearing before a parliamentary health committee on Tuesday, Wednesday 31, said the government was trying to put in place similar measures for private companies importing other approved vaccines.

"COVAX has put aside money so that if somebody suffers major permanent damage or if one, unfortunately, dies as a result of the vaccination process there will be compensation," he said.

He further confirmed that the Russian-developed vaccine Sputnik V was indeed in the country and had received emergency use authorisation by the Pharmacy and Poisons Board (PPB).

"Yes, this vaccine has been granted emergency use authorization by the Pharmacy and Poisons Board (PPB) like other vaccines that we have received through the COVAX platform," he said.

According to Amoth, the government had signed an indemnification process with the COVID-19 vaccine manufacturers that will see the compensation effected if need be.

Speaking at the same committee, the director-general said the vaccine had not been pre-qualified by World Health Organisation (WHO).

“Any pre-qualified vaccine approval takes less than a week for emergency authorisation use, it takes a longer time for a vaccine that is not pre-qualified by the WHO, since there are set out steps that have to be followed for such an approval,” Amoth said.

He, however, noted that there is a possibility of a vaccine being used without approval by the WHO, provided it had passed other stringent tests.

Meanwhile, the Russian Embassy in Kenya in a statement dated March 29, said the vaccine had been imported for use in Kenya by a private entity for commercial purposes.

It advised the entities to follow all government guidelines and rules issued by the Ministry of Health.

At the same time, the embassy asked all concerned parties to direct all questions related to the private commercial importation to its importers in the country.

Those interested in getting the vaccine would have to part with KSh 11,000 for the requisite two doses, which would be administered 21 days apart.

The vaccine that was initially met with scepticism has been deployed to at least 60 countries globally and in Africa, it has been deployed in Algeria, Congo and Tunisia.
