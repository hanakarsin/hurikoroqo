Opinion: Media Should Give Kenyans a Break, Negative News Coverage Depressing Many

A recent survey seeking to find out if Kenyans greatly value television news nowadays produced some interesting results.

Many respondents unequivocally admitted that they no longer look forward to primetime news, as it is depressing.

It is not hard to see why news bulletins are nowadays laced with all manner of toxins, from murders to homicides, pandemics, corruption and impunity.

Newscasts rarely give us anything to smile about these days.

The case is even worse when it comes to international media. Not long ago, CNN, a leading international media house, broadcast a sensational feature on COVID-19 and vaccination uptake in Nyeri county.

The media outlet appeared to overstate the number of fatalities recorded per day, gleefully focusing on the coffin-making business and giving the misleading notion that many people remain unvaccinated because they don’t want to.

It is common knowledge that many Kenyans could not access the vaccine then, and long queues characterized the desperate quest for the jab across the country.

Predictably, CNN could not tell the world that Kenya had just successfully hosted over 100 countries during the World Athletics U20 Championships and led the charts in the final medal count.

It would not tell us that COVID-19 protocols were well observed, and there was no single coronavirus scare at the event.

It was not the first time such bias was manifested. Sometime back, CNN inexplicably referred to Kenya as a ‘hotbed of terror.

In the wake of former President Barack Obama’s visit to Kenya, a strapline on CNN’s news item about security fears on the high-profile trip, presented by Pentagon Correspondent Barbara Starr, read;

“President Barack Obama is not just heading to his father’s homeland, but to a region that’s a hotbed of terror.”

At the onset of 2019, following the deadly terrorist attack at an upmarket hotel complex in Kenya’s capital that claimed 15 lives and leftover 30 injured, the New York Times ran photos of victims covered in blood, something they wouldn’t do if the attack happened somewhere in the West.

After an Ethiopian Airlines plane crashed and killed all 157 passengers in 2019, major Western media houses highlighted the names and countries of victims from the West but failed to mention those from African countries - including Kenya that lost 32 citizens (the highest death toll for a single country in the crash).

Western media’s ignorance and ill-conceived prejudice against Kenya and Africa must be called out for what it is.

Its zealous yet injudicious portrayal of Kenya as a country of unending horrors, only known for poverty, diseases and conflict, must be tamed.

Like any other country, Kenya has its good and its blemishes.

As much as the sad stories are narrated with potency, the great positive stories must also be delivered with equal vigour.

The bias notwithstanding, Kenya is a great nation, and citizens must grow a thick skin and resist being drawn to the deliberate negativity towards the country.

It is nice to remind ourselves that despite all the negative coverage out there, we are a more advanced country than most of our counterparts and that our innovative Kenyan lifestyle is one of a kind.

Negative news sells

Undoubtedly, negative news sells. People feel better about their lives when they know that others have bigger problems than them.

A European who’s unhappy he can't get a mortgage will, however unconsciously, likely see his life in brighter lights after watching footage of people from Africa with no electricity, no running water, and little food to eat.

As we head towards the general elections, the Kenyan media has also continued to be plagued by both ethical and professional failures.

Therefore, Kenyans must not be lost in this negativity spewed all over by selfish individuals and their cohorts.

However, besides COVID-19, joblessness, and a high cost of living, there is always something that each of us can be happy about.

It is time we learn to always look on the bright side of life and see the glass as half full, not half empty.

Researchers are finding more and more evidence pointing to the benefits of optimism and positive thinking.

Findings suggest that not only are positive thinkers healthier and less stressed, they also have greater overall well-being.

James Allen describes one of the best analogies on the positivity that I have ever read in his timeless classic “As a Man Thinketh.”

He likens the mind to a garden. If planted with beautiful plants and carefully nurtured and tended to, the garden will yield beautiful flowers and be a place of comfort, beauty, and peace.

If neglected, weeds will creep in and destroy the beauty and yield thorns, destruction, and harshness.

Of course, some twisted or strange gardener may actually deliberately plant weeds and unpleasant plants.

It is the same with the mind. If you plant beautiful, healthy thoughts into your mind and embrace a positive outlook on life, you will reap a beautiful, healthy life.

If you plant negative thoughts, you will reap destruction and despair in your life.

If you neglect to feed your mind healthy thoughts and protect it from negative thoughts, the weeds of destruction will creep in and choke your mind.

It is easy to drift to gloom and despair without knowing all the uncertainties brought about by COVID-19 and the uncertain political future.

We must consciously embrace positivity, look out for good in every situation, and focus ahead.

Failure to appreciate the good things around us, and making it a national pastime to complain endlessly, will do us more harm than good.

Kenyans must also stand up to forces that recklessly tarnish the country’s image internationally.

The same unique Kenyan spirit caused CNN’s Global Executive Vice President and Managing Director, Tony Maddox, to fly all the way to Nairobi to apologise to President Uhuru Kenyatta for the ‘hotbed of terror’ slur should not be left to die.

A strong spirit proves that nothing is insurmountable if the people join hands and speak in one voice against a common enemy.
